# Atrial Fibrillation (AF): A Comprehensive Overview

## Definition

Atrial fibrillation is a supraventricular tachyarrhythmia characterized by uncoordinated atrial activation and ineffective atrial contraction. It is the most common type of cardiac arrhythmia encountered in clinical practice.

---

## ECG Characteristics

### Hallmark Features

- **Absent P waves**: Cannot identify normal, regular P waves; replaced by fibrillatory (f) waves — irregular, chaotic baseline oscillations at 350-600/min
- **Irregularly irregular R-R intervals**: QRS complexes occur at completely irregular intervals with no pattern to ventricular response (unless concurrent AV block)
- **Narrow QRS complex**: Usually <120 ms (unless bundle branch block or aberrant conduction)

### Ventricular Rate

Variable ventricular rate: Typically 100-160 bpm if untreated (rapid ventricular response); <100 bpm with rate control or AV nodal disease.

### Fibrillatory Wave Types

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Coarse AF** | Larger f waves (>1 mm), often seen in early/paroxysmal AF; may indicate better response to cardioversion |
| **Fine AF** | Smaller f waves, associated with longer-standing AF and more extensive atrial remodeling |

---

## Pathophysiology

### Triggers (Initiation)

Abnormal electrical signals that initiate AF episodes.

**Pulmonary Veins (Primary Trigger)**: Myocardial sleeves at the pulmonary vein-left atrial junction contain cells prone to ectopic firing. When these generate abnormal signals that propagate into the atrium, they can initiate AF.

**Non-pulmonary Vein Triggers (Less Common)**:
- Superior vena cava
- Coronary sinus
- Left atrial posterior wall
- Crista terminalis

### Substrate (Maintenance)

**Structural Remodeling**: Fibrosis from long-term pressure (hypertension, heart failure) causes atrial dilation, myocyte death, and replacement by fibrous tissue. This impedes electrical conduction, slows signal transmission, disrupts conduction pathways, and facilitates reentry circuits.

**Electrical Remodeling**: Ion channel changes (calcium and potassium channels) shorten the refractory period. The longer AF persists, the more atrial electrical properties adapt to this chaotic rhythm, making termination increasingly difficult — "AF begets AF."

### Modulators

**Autonomic Nervous System**: Sympathetic-parasympathetic imbalance (exercise-induced sympathetic activation or post-meal/sleep vagal predominance) can alter atrial electrical properties and trigger AF.

**Ganglionated Plexi**: Overactive ganglionated plexi surrounding the heart may also initiate AF episodes.

---

## Clinical Presentation

### Asymptomatic (Silent AF)

Up to 30% of patients are asymptomatic. AF is discovered incidentally during routine examinations or, unfortunately, only after a stroke occurs.

### Common Symptoms

- Fatigue: Most common nonspecific symptom
- Palpitations: Sensation of rapid or irregular heartbeats
- Exercise intolerance: From loss of atrial contribution to cardiac output
- Dyspnea: Especially with rapid ventricular response or underlying heart failure
- Chest discomfort: May mimic angina
- Dizziness/Presyncope: From reduced cardiac output or pauses

### Serious Presentations

- Stroke/TIA: May be first presentation in ~25% of AF-related strokes
- Heart failure: Acute decompensation or new-onset tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy

## Classification & Staging

The ACC/AHA/ACCP/HRS staging system views AF as a continuous disease spectrum rather than isolated events. Early intervention on risk factors may prevent or delay AF onset, and even permanent AF requires ongoing anticoagulation and risk factor management.

### Stage 1: At Risk for AF

Presence of modifiable and nonmodifiable risk factors associated with AF, but no AF detected.

**Modifiable Risk Factors**: Obesity, Lack of fitness, Hypertension, Sleep apnea, Alcohol, Diabetes

**Nonmodifiable Risk Factors**: Genetics, Male sex, Age

### Stage 2: Pre-AF

Evidence of structural or electrical findings predisposing to AF (atrial enlargement, frequent atrial ectopy, short bursts of atrial tachycardia, atrial flutter), but no clinical AF yet.

### Stage 3: AF

Confirmed AF with substages based on duration and pattern. Patients may transition among different substages.

| Substage | Name | Description |
|----------|------|-------------|
| 3A | Paroxysmal AF | Intermittent AF that terminates within ≤7 days of onset |
| 3B | Persistent AF | Continuous AF that sustains for >7 days and requires intervention |
| 3C | Long-standing Persistent AF | Continuous AF for >12 months in duration |
| 3D | Successful AF Ablation | Freedom from AF after percutaneous or surgical intervention to eliminate AF |

### Stage 4: Permanent AF

No further attempts at rhythm control after discussion between patient and clinician. Focus shifts to rate control and stroke prevention.

## Treatment

### Management Frameworks

The 2020 ESC guidelines introduced the ABC pathway (Anticoagulation, Better symptom control, Cardiovascular risk). The 2024 ESC guidelines updated this to the AF-CARE pathway (Comorbidities, Avoid stroke, Reduce symptoms, Evaluation). The 2023 ACC/AHA guidelines organize management into similar domains without a specific acronym.

---

### A – Avoid Stroke and Thromboembolism

#### Stroke Risk Assessment

**2023 ACC/AHA Guidelines**: Recommend anticoagulation based on annual stroke risk ≥2%, which corresponds to CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥2 in men and ≥3 in women. Female sex is treated as a risk modifier rather than independent risk factor.

**2024 ESC Guidelines**: Adopted CHA₂DS₂-VA score (sex category removed). Anticoagulation recommended for all patients with CHA₂DS₂-VA ≥2 regardless of sex. Score of 1 = Class IIa recommendation (may be considered).

#### Anticoagulant Selection

**DOACs (Preferred)**: Direct oral anticoagulants have lower intracranial bleeding risk compared to warfarin.
- Dabigatran
- Rivaroxaban
- Apixaban
- Edoxaban

**Warfarin (VKA)**: Target INR 2.0-3.0. Remains appropriate for mechanical heart valves or moderate-to-severe mitral stenosis.

#### Key Points

- Antiplatelet therapy is NOT an alternative to anticoagulation (Class III)
- Temporal pattern of AF (paroxysmal vs persistent) does NOT affect anticoagulation decisions — paroxysmal AF carries similar stroke risk

---

### R – Rate Control

**Goal**: Prevent tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy with target heart rate <100-110 bpm for most patients. Stricter control (<80 bpm at rest, <110 during exercise) may be appropriate for patients with heart failure or suspected AF-induced cardiomyopathy.

#### Agents

**Beta-blockers (First-line)**:
- Bisoprolol
- Carvedilol
- Metoprolol
- Nebivolol

*Caution*: Use cautiously in asthma patients

**Non-DHP Calcium Channel Blockers**:
- Diltiazem
- Verapamil

*Contraindication*: Contraindicated in HFrEF due to negative inotropic effects

**Digoxin**: Adjunctive agent, particularly for sedentary elderly patients or those with heart failure

---

### R – Rhythm Control

**Goal**: Restore and maintain sinus rhythm

#### Catheter Ablation

For selected patients with symptomatic paroxysmal AF (generally younger with few comorbidities), catheter ablation is recommended as first-line therapy (Class I). For broader population of symptomatic paroxysmal or persistent AF, catheter ablation as first-line therapy can be useful (Class IIa).

#### Long-term Antiarrhythmic Drug Selection

**No Structural Heart Disease**:
| Drug | Class | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| Flecainide | Ic | When using Class Ic drugs, concurrent beta-blocker use is advised to prevent potential atrial flutter with rapid ventricular response |
| Propafenone | Ic | Same precaution as Flecainide |
| Dronedarone | III | Alternative option |

**Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)**:
| Drug | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Dronedarone | Preferred for favorable side effect profile |
| Sotalol | Requires QT monitoring |
| Amiodarone | Last resort due to long-term toxicity |

*Contraindicated*: Flecainide/Propafenone contraindicated due to proarrhythmic risk

**Heart Failure (especially HFrEF)**:
| Drug | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Amiodarone | Primary option for HFrEF |

*Contraindicated*: Dronedarone contraindicated — increases mortality (ANDROMEDA trial)

**Important**: Dronedarone is also contraindicated in permanent AF (PALLAS trial showed increased cardiovascular events)

#### Acute Pharmacological Cardioversion

**Hemodynamically stable, no structural heart disease**: Flecainide or Propafenone (PO or IV)

**Pill-in-the-Pocket Strategy**: For select patients with paroxysmal AF and no structural heart disease: after initial safety evaluation in a monitored setting, patients may self-administer a single high dose of Flecainide or Propafenone at episode onset.

**Structural heart disease or heart failure**: Amiodarone (IV): Slower onset but safer cardioversion

---

### C – Comorbidity and Risk Factor Management

| Condition | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Hypertension | BP target 120-129/70-79 mmHg |
| Obesity | Weight reduction ≥10% for BMI ≥30 |
| Alcohol | ≤30 g/week, complete abstinence preferred |
| Diabetes / Heart Failure | Prioritize SGLT2 inhibitors |
| Obstructive Sleep Apnea | Management to minimize apneic episodes |
| Physical Activity | Tailored exercise programme for regular moderate/vigorous activity |

---

## Monitoring & Follow-up

### E – Evaluation and Dynamic Reassessment

**Timeline**: Initial comprehensive evaluation 6 months after diagnosis, then at least annually. Immediate evaluation if stroke, bleeding, or hospitalization for cardiac issues occurs.

**Assessment Items**:
- Stroke and bleeding risk
- Symptom and treatment efficacy
- Comorbidity control
- Disease progression

---

## Key Clinical Principles

1. AF is a dynamic disease spectrum requiring individualized, multidisciplinary care

2. Temporal pattern (paroxysmal, persistent, permanent) guides symptom management but does NOT determine anticoagulation needs

3. Risk factor modification remains crucial across all stages — even permanent AF patients benefit from aggressive management of hypertension, obesity, sleep apnea

4. Integrated management through collaboration among primary care, cardiology, pharmacy, nursing optimizes outcomes through patient-centered shared decision-making
