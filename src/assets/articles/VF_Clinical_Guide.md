# Ventricular Fibrillation (VF) - Clinical Reference Guide

---

## Tab 0: Overview

### Definition & Clinical Significance

Ventricular fibrillation represents the most lethal of all cardiac arrhythmias—a state of complete electrical chaos in which the ventricles quiver ineffectively rather than contracting in coordinated fashion to pump blood. This chaotic electrical activity, characterized by disorganized depolarization waves traversing the myocardium at rates of **300-500 per minute**, renders the heart incapable of generating any meaningful cardiac output. Without blood flow to the brain and vital organs, consciousness is lost within seconds, and irreversible neurological injury begins within 4-6 minutes.

**Key Points:**
- VF is responsible for the majority of sudden cardiac deaths
- Accounts for the initial rhythm in **65-85% of out-of-hospital cardiac arrests**
- VF is a **"shockable" rhythm**—the only definitive treatment is electrical defibrillation
- Survival decreases by **7-10% for each minute** without defibrillation
- AED use within 3 minutes can achieve survival rates up to **95%**

### Clinical Presentation

> ⚠️ **IMMEDIATE INTERVENTION REQUIRED: VF = CARDIAC ARREST**

The clinical presentation of VF is that of cardiac arrest—there is no stable or hemodynamically tolerated form of this arrhythmia.

| Sign | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Loss of Consciousness** | Sudden, within 10-20 seconds of VF onset |
| **Pulse** | Absent |
| **Blood Pressure** | Absent/unobtainable |
| **Heart Sounds** | Absent |
| **Respirations** | Agonal gasping → Complete apnea |
| **Unwitnessed** | Found unresponsive |

### ECG Characteristics

The electrocardiographic appearance of VF is unmistakable:

| Parameter | Characteristic |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Rate** | 300-500 bpm (unmeasurable) |
| **Rhythm** | Grossly irregular, chaotic |
| **P Waves** | Absent |
| **QRS Complex** | No identifiable QRS; irregular fibrillatory waves |
| **T Waves** | Absent |

#### VF Subtypes by Amplitude

| Type | Amplitude | Clinical Significance |
|------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Coarse VF** | ≥3 mm waveforms | Early phase; relatively preserved myocardial energy; **better response to defibrillation** |
| **Fine VF** | <3 mm waveforms | Prolonged phase; depleted energy stores; may mimic asystole; **poorer prognosis** |

> **Note:** As VF persists untreated, it invariably degenerates from coarse → fine → asystole as myocardial ATP is exhausted.

## Tab 1: Etiology

### Primary (Idiopathic) VF: Genetic Channelopathies

Primary VF occurs in structurally normal hearts due to inherited disorders of cardiac ion channels:

| Channelopathy | Mechanism | Key Features |
|---------------|-----------|--------------|
| **Long QT Syndrome (LQTS)** | K⁺ or Na⁺ channel mutations → prolonged repolarization | Torsades de Pointes → VF; triggered by stress, exercise, or medications |
| **Brugada Syndrome** | Na⁺ channel dysfunction | ST elevation V1-V3; VF risk especially during sleep or fever |
| **CPVT** | Ryanodine receptor/calsequestrin mutations → Ca²⁺ dysregulation | Bidirectional VT → VF triggered by exercise or emotion; affects young patients |
| **Short QT Syndrome** | Abbreviated repolarization | Reentrant VF; very rare |

### Secondary VF: Structural Heart Disease

The majority of VF occurs in the context of structural cardiac abnormalities:

| Condition | Arrhythmogenic Mechanism |
|-----------|-------------------------|
| **Ischemic Cardiomyopathy / MI** | Scar tissue creates reentrant circuits; acute ischemia disrupts electrical propagation |
| **Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)** | Disorganized myocardial fibers; abnormal intramural coronaries; fibrosis |
| **Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM)** | Myocardial stretch; fibrosis; neurohormonal activation |
| **ARVC** | Fibrofatty replacement of RV myocardium; high SCD risk |

### Electrolyte Abnormalities

| Abnormality | Effect on Myocardium |
|-------------|---------------------|
| **Hypokalemia** | Prolongs repolarization → triggered arrhythmias |
| **Hyperkalemia** | Depresses conduction → VF or asystole |
| **Hypomagnesemia** | Predisposes to afterdepolarizations |

### Metabolic & Systemic Factors

| Factor | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **Acidosis** | Depresses myocardial contractility and conduction |
| **Hypoxemia** | Creates ischemic substrate |
| **Hypothermia** | Predisposes to VF; renders myocardium refractory to defibrillation |

## Tab 2: Treatment (ACLS Protocol)

### Immediate Actions

The management of VF follows the **cardiac arrest chain of survival**:

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| **1** | Call for help & activate EMS |
| **2** | Start CPR immediately |
| **3** | Defibrillate AS SOON AS POSSIBLE |

> ⏱️ **Time is critical:** Every minute without defibrillation reduces survival by 7-10%

---

### Defibrillation — The Definitive Treatment

Defibrillation is the **ONLY** intervention that can terminate VF.

#### Energy Settings

| Waveform | Energy |
|----------|--------|
| **Biphasic** | 120-200 J (device-specific) |
| **Monophasic** | 360 J |

#### Protocol

1. Defibrillate **AS SOON AS** VF is identified (unsynchronized shock)
2. Resume CPR **IMMEDIATELY** after shock (do not pause to check rhythm)
3. Continue CPR for **2 minutes**
4. Rhythm check → Shock again if VF persists
5. Consider **escalating energy** for subsequent shocks if device allows

> **Refractory VF Options:**
> - Vector change (anterolateral → anteroposterior pad position)
> - Double sequential defibrillation (uncertain benefit per 2025 guidelines)

---

### High-Quality CPR

| Parameter | Standard |
|-----------|----------|
| **Rate** | 100-120 compressions/minute |
| **Depth** | At least 5 cm (2 inches) |
| **Recoil** | Allow full chest recoil between compressions |
| **Interruptions** | Minimize; pause <10 seconds for rhythm checks |
| **Rotation** | Change compressors every 2 minutes |

> **ETCO₂ Monitoring:** Values >10 mmHg indicate adequate CPR quality

---

### Pharmacotherapy

Medications are **adjunctive** to defibrillation, not a substitute.

#### Epinephrine (Vasopressor)

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Dose** | 1 mg IV/IO |
| **Timing** | Every 3-5 minutes |
| **When to start** | After initial defibrillation attempts fail (typically after 2nd shock) |
| **Mechanism** | α-adrenergic vasoconstriction → ↑ coronary and cerebral perfusion |

#### Amiodarone (Antiarrhythmic — First Choice)

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| **First Dose** | 300 mg IV/IO bolus |
| **Second Dose** | 150 mg IV/IO |
| **Timing** | After 3rd shock for refractory VF/pVT |
| **Evidence** | Improves ROSC and survival to hospital admission |

> ⚠️ **Note:** Neither amiodarone nor lidocaine has demonstrated improvement in **neurologically intact survival**

#### Lidocaine (Alternative to Amiodarone)

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| **First Dose** | 1-1.5 mg/kg IV/IO |
| **Subsequent Doses** | 0.5-0.75 mg/kg |
| **Use** | When amiodarone unavailable or as alternative |

---

### Address Reversible Causes (H's and T's)

Systematic evaluation throughout resuscitation is essential:

| H's | T's |
|-----|-----|
| **H**ypovolemia | **T**ension pneumothorax |
| **H**ypoxia | **T**amponade (cardiac) |
| **H**ydrogen ion (acidosis) | **T**oxins |
| **H**ypo/hyperkalemia | **T**hrombosis (coronary or pulmonary) |
| **H**ypothermia | |

**Diagnostic Considerations:**
- Point-of-care ultrasound for tamponade, PE, hypovolemia
- Empiric calcium chloride if hyperkalemia suspected
- Thrombolytics for suspected massive PE
- Needle decompression for tension pneumothorax

## Tab 3: Prevention

### Primary Prevention (High-Risk Patients Without Prior VF)

**Goal:** Prevent sudden cardiac death from VF before it occurs

#### Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator (ICD)

The ICD continuously monitors heart rhythm and automatically delivers defibrillation if VF is detected—acting as an internal safety net.

#### Indications for Primary Prevention ICD

| Indication | Rationale |
|------------|-----------|
| **EF ≤35% + NYHA Class II-III HF** | Severely reduced pumping function increases fatal arrhythmia risk |
| **Prior MI with EF ≤30%** | Scar tissue creates arrhythmogenic substrate for VF |
| **Inherited Channelopathies** (LQTS, Brugada, CPVT) | Ion channel mutations predispose to spontaneous VF |
| **HCM with risk factors** | Abnormal myocardial architecture promotes reentrant VF circuits |
| **ARVC with risk factors** | Fibrofatty replacement creates VF substrate |

---

### Secondary Prevention (Survivors of VF Arrest)

**Goal:** Reduce VF recurrence and prevent sudden cardiac death

#### Management Strategies

| Strategy | Rationale |
|----------|-----------|
| **ICD Implantation** (Class I) | Survivors are at high risk of repeat arrest; ICD provides automatic detection and termination of VF |
| **Antiarrhythmic Therapy** (Amiodarone, Sotalol) | Chronic oral therapy to suppress arrhythmic triggers and reduce VF recurrence |
| **Beta-blockers** | Reduce sympathetic triggers that precipitate VF |
| **Treat Underlying Heart Disease** | Revascularization, HF optimization reduces arrhythmic substrate |
| **Electrolyte Optimization** | Maintain K⁺ ≥4.0 mEq/L and Mg²⁺ ≥2.0 mg/dL |

---

### Antiarrhythmic Medications for Long-Term VF Prevention

> **Note:** These agents are used chronically to reduce recurrent VF—NOT for acute VF termination

| Agent | Efficacy | Considerations |
|-------|----------|----------------|
| **Amiodarone** | Most effective for long-term VF suppression | Significant toxicity: thyroid, pulmonary, hepatic; requires monitoring |
| **Sotalol** | Moderate long-term efficacy | Risk of QT prolongation and Torsades de Pointes |
| **Beta-blockers** | Reduces sympathetic triggers | Adjunct therapy; not standalone VF prevention; mortality benefit in HF |

## Quick Reference: VF Management Algorithm

```
VF IDENTIFIED
     ↓
DEFIBRILLATE IMMEDIATELY (Biphasic 120-200J / Monophasic 360J)
     ↓
CPR × 2 minutes (100-120/min, ≥5cm depth)
     ↓
RHYTHM CHECK
     ↓
If VF persists → SHOCK #2 → CPR × 2 min → EPINEPHRINE 1mg
     ↓
RHYTHM CHECK
     ↓
If VF persists → SHOCK #3 → CPR × 2 min → AMIODARONE 300mg (or Lidocaine)
     ↓
Continue cycle: Shock → CPR → Epi q3-5min → Consider Amiodarone 150mg
     ↓
Address H's and T's throughout
```

## Key Differences: VF vs VT

| Feature | Ventricular Fibrillation | Ventricular Tachycardia |
|---------|-------------------------|------------------------|
| **Rhythm** | Chaotic, disorganized | Usually regular |
| **QRS** | No identifiable QRS | Wide QRS (≥120 ms) |
| **Rate** | 300-500 bpm (unmeasurable) | 100-250 bpm |
| **Hemodynamics** | Always pulseless (cardiac arrest) | May be stable, unstable, or pulseless |
| **Treatment** | Always unsynchronized defibrillation | Depends on hemodynamic status |
