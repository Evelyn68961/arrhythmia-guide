# Ventricular Tachycardia (VT) - Clinical Reference Guide

---

## Tab 0: Overview

### Definition & Clinical Significance

Ventricular tachycardia represents one of the most clinically consequential cardiac arrhythmias, defined electrocardiographically as **three or more consecutive ventricular beats** at a rate exceeding **100 beats per minute** with a **QRS duration of at least 120 milliseconds**. This wide-complex tachycardia originates below the bundle of His, producing the characteristic broad QRS morphology that distinguishes it from supraventricular arrhythmias with aberrant conduction. The clinical presentation spans a remarkable spectrum—from entirely asymptomatic episodes detected incidentally on monitoring to sudden cardiac death—making rapid recognition and appropriate management essential for patient survival.

### Clinical Presentation Stratification

The hemodynamic tolerance of VT serves as the primary determinant of immediate management strategy:

| Hemodynamic Status | Clinical Features |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Stable** | Palpitations, chest discomfort, dyspnea, lightheadedness with preserved BP and cerebral perfusion |
| **Unstable** | Hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg), altered mental status, acute pulmonary edema, ischemic chest pain, cardiogenic shock |
| **Pulseless** | Cardiac arrest rhythm requiring immediate CPR and defibrillation; mortality >90% without prompt treatment |

### ECG Recognition

The electrocardiographic hallmarks of VT include:

- **Rate**: 100-250 bpm
- **Rhythm**: Regular (or nearly regular) wide-complex rhythm
- **QRS Duration**: ≥120 ms with morphology reflecting ventricular origin
- **AV Dissociation**: Strong evidence for ventricular origin when present
- **Capture/Fusion Beats**: May occasionally interrupt the tachycardia
- **Morphology Patterns**:
  - LBBB pattern → Right ventricular origin
  - RBBB pattern → Left ventricular origin

## Tab 1: Etiology & Pathophysiology

### Structural Heart Disease: The Predominant Substrate

Coronary artery disease with prior myocardial infarction constitutes the most common etiology of sustained VT worldwide, accounting for approximately **80% of cases** in developed countries. The pathologic substrate involves scar tissue from healed infarction, which creates zones of slow conduction bordered by areas of complete conduction block—the anatomical requirements for reentrant circuits.

| Structural Cause | Mechanism |
|-----------------|-----------|
| **CAD/Prior MI** | Scar-related reentry circuits |
| **Dilated Cardiomyopathy** | Myocardial fibrosis, adverse remodeling |
| **Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy** | High SCD risk, especially in young athletes |
| **ARVC** | Fibrofatty replacement of RV myocardium |

### Channelopathies and Idiopathic VT

In the absence of structural heart disease, VT may arise from inherited ion channel abnormalities or from focal automaticity in structurally normal hearts:

| Condition | Characteristic Features |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **Long QT Syndrome** | Torsades de Pointes from delayed repolarization |
| **Brugada Syndrome** | ST elevation V1-V3; polymorphic VT risk |
| **CPVT** | Bidirectional VT triggered by exercise/stress |
| **Idiopathic RVOT VT** | Benign; excellent ablation success rates |

### Mechanistic Foundations

Three fundamental electrophysiological mechanisms underlie ventricular tachyarrhythmias:

1. **Reentry** (Most Common in Structural Heart Disease)
   - Requires heterogeneous conduction with slow conduction zones and unidirectional block
   - Permits circular propagation of activation wavefront

2. **Triggered Activity**
   - **EADs (Early Afterdepolarizations)**: Phases 2-3; QT prolongation → Torsades
   - **DADs (Delayed Afterdepolarizations)**: Phase 4; Ca²⁺ overload (digitalis, CPVT)

3. **Enhanced Automaticity**
   - Abnormal spontaneous depolarization
   - Seen in ischemia, electrolyte derangements

## Tab 2: Classification

### Temporal Classification: Prognostic Implications

| Type | Definition | Clinical Significance |
|------|------------|----------------------|
| **Non-Sustained VT (NSVT)** | <30 seconds, self-terminating | Risk marker for SCD in structural heart disease; may guide EP testing/ICD |
| **Sustained VT** | ≥30 seconds OR requires intervention | Demands treatment and comprehensive evaluation |

### Morphological Classification: Mechanistic Insights

| Type | QRS Characteristics | Typical Etiology |
|------|---------------------|------------------|
| **Monomorphic VT** | Uniform beat-to-beat | Stable reentrant circuit (post-MI scar); better hemodynamic tolerance |
| **Polymorphic VT** | Continuously varying | Acute ischemia, channelopathy, electrolyte derangement |
| **Torsades de Pointes** | Sinusoidal twisting around baseline | QT prolongation (congenital or acquired) |

## Tab 3: Treatment (Acute Management)

### The Fundamental Principle: Hemodynamic Assessment First

| Presentation | Immediate Action |
|--------------|------------------|
| **Pulseless VT** | CPR + Defibrillation (unsynchronized) |
| **Unstable VT with pulse** | Synchronized cardioversion (sedate if possible) |
| **Stable sustained VT** | Pharmacological cardioversion or elective cardioversion |

### Pulseless VT: ACLS Protocol

**Shockable Rhythm Algorithm:**

1. **Defibrillation** (unsynchronized)
   - Biphasic: 120-200 J (or manufacturer recommendation)
   - Monophasic: 360 J

2. **High-quality CPR** × 2 minutes

3. **Epinephrine** 1 mg IV/IO after 2nd shock, then every 3-5 minutes

4. **Antiarrhythmic** after 3rd shock:
   - **Amiodarone** 300 mg IV bolus → 150 mg if needed
   - OR **Lidocaine** 1-1.5 mg/kg → 0.5-0.75 mg/kg

5. Address reversible causes (Hs and Ts)

> **Note**: Neither amiodarone nor lidocaine has demonstrated improvement in neurologically intact survival.

### Stable Sustained VT: Pharmacological Options

| Agent | Dosing | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| **Procainamide** (Preferred) | 10 mg/kg over 20-30 min | PROCAMIO trial: Superior efficacy (67% vs 38%) and safety vs amiodarone; 2022 ESC Guidelines favor this agent |
| **Amiodarone** | 150 mg over 10 min, repeat as needed | Alternative when procainamide unavailable |
| **Lidocaine** | 1-1.5 mg/kg | Inferior efficacy; fallen out of favor |

**Critical**: Apply defibrillator pads before antiarrhythmic administration.

### Electrical Cardioversion

- **Monomorphic VT**: Synchronized cardioversion, start 100 J biphasic, escalate as needed
- **Polymorphic VT**: Unsynchronized defibrillation (synchronization may fail)
- **Sedation**: Propofol, etomidate, or midazolam when hemodynamically stable

### Addressing Reversible Factors

| Factor | Target/Action |
|--------|---------------|
| Potassium | Maintain >4.0 mEq/L |
| Magnesium | Maintain >2.0 mg/dL |
| Acute ischemia | Urgent coronary evaluation/revascularization |
| QT-prolonging drugs | Discontinue offending agents |
| Digitalis toxicity | Digoxin immune Fab (Digibind) |

## Tab 4: Prevention (Long-Term Management)

### Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator (ICD)

#### Secondary Prevention (Class I)
- Survivors of sustained VT or VF **not due to completely reversible cause**
- Landmark trials: AVID, CIDS, CASH

#### Primary Prevention (Class I)

| Indication | Criteria |
|------------|----------|
| Ischemic/Non-ischemic CM | LVEF ≤35% + NYHA II-III + ≥3 months GDMT |
| Post-MI | LVEF ≤30% (even NYHA Class I) |
| High-risk Cardiomyopathies | HCM/ARVC with risk factors; LMNA, PLN, FLNC, RBM20 mutations |

### Antiarrhythmic Drug Therapy

| Agent | Role | Considerations |
|-------|------|----------------|
| **Amiodarone** | Most effective for VT recurrence/ICD shock reduction | Long-term toxicity (thyroid, pulmonary, hepatic) |
| **Sotalol** | β-blocker + Class III effects | Proarrhythmic risk; requires QT monitoring |
| **Beta-blockers** | First-line adjunct | Mortality benefit in heart failure |
| **Mexiletine** | Adjunct to amiodarone | Useful in refractory cases |

### Catheter Ablation

| Indication | Recommendation |
|------------|----------------|
| **Idiopathic RVOT/Fascicular VT** | First-line therapy; >90% cure rate |
| **Scar-related VT with preserved EF (≥40%)** | Alternative to ICD (2022 ESC) |
| **Recurrent ICD shocks despite AAD** | Reduces shock burden, improves QOL |
| **VT storm** | Urgent ablation consideration |
| **Drug-refractory VT** | Strong indication |

### Novel and Refractory VT Management

| Modality | Description | Indications |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **STAR** (Stereotactic Arrhythmia Radioablation) | Non-invasive focused radiation to ablate arrhythmogenic tissue | Failed catheter ablation; not candidates for invasive procedures |
| **LCSD** (Left Cardiac Sympathetic Denervation) | Surgical interruption of left stellate ganglion | CPVT, LQTS with events despite β-blockade, refractory VT storm |

## Quick Reference: VT Management Summary

| Scenario | Immediate Action |
|----------|------------------|
| **Pulseless VT** | CPR + Defibrillation (unsync) + Epinephrine + Amiodarone/Lidocaine |
| **Unstable VT with pulse** | Synchronized cardioversion (sedate if possible) |
| **Stable monomorphic VT** | Procainamide (preferred) or Amiodarone or elective cardioversion |
| **Polymorphic VT (normal QT)** | Treat as ischemia; β-blockers; consider amiodarone |
| **Torsades de Pointes** | Magnesium IV; stop QT drugs; consider pacing; **AVOID** amiodarone/sotalol/procainamide |
