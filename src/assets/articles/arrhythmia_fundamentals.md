# Arrhythmia Fundamentals

# Part 1: Foundational Cardiac Electrophysiology Concepts

## Overview

Understanding cardiac arrhythmias requires mastery of three foundational concepts: the cardiac conduction system anatomy, cardiac action potential and ion channel physiology, and the Vaughan-Williams classification of antiarrhythmic drugs. These concepts directly correlate with ECG findings and guide pharmacological management.

---

## 1. Cardiac Conduction System

The heart's electrical conduction pathway determines cardiac rhythm and sequence. Electrical impulses originate from the SA node and propagate through a specialized conduction network to coordinate atrial and ventricular contraction.

### Conduction Pathway Sequence

The SA node, located at the junction of the superior vena cava and right atrium, serves as the heart's natural pacemaker, generating spontaneous electrical impulses at 60-100 beats per minute. These impulses spread through atrial muscle, causing atrial depolarization and contraction, which corresponds to the P wave on ECG.

The AV node receives the impulse and delays transmission, allowing complete atrial contraction before ventricular filling. This delay corresponds to the PR interval. The AV node also protects the ventricles from excessively rapid atrial rates and serves as a backup pacemaker at 40-60 bpm if the SA node fails.

From the AV node, impulses travel through the Bundle of His, then divide into the right and left bundle branches. The left bundle branch further divides into anterior and posterior fascicles. Finally, the Purkinje fibers rapidly conduct impulses throughout the ventricular myocardium, enabling synchronized ventricular contraction. The Purkinje system can serve as a last-resort pacemaker at 20-40 bpm.

### Pacemaker Hierarchy

If a higher pacemaker fails, lower sites take over at progressively slower rates. This hierarchy ensures cardiac rhythm continuity: SA node (60-100 bpm) → AV node (40-60 bpm) → His-Purkinje (20-40 bpm). The escape rhythm rate depends on the level of the backup pacemaker.

## 2. Action Potential and Ion Channels

Ion movement across cardiac cell membranes generates the action potential, which directly correlates with ECG waveforms. Understanding ion channel function explains both normal cardiac electrophysiology and the mechanisms of antiarrhythmic drugs.

### Action Potential Phases

Phase 4 is the resting phase, where potassium leak channels maintain a stable resting membrane potential of approximately -90 mV.

Phase 0 represents rapid depolarization. Sodium influx through voltage-gated sodium channels causes the membrane potential to rise rapidly from -90 mV to +20 mV. This phase corresponds to the QRS complex on ECG, representing ventricular depolarization. Sodium channel blockade slows depolarization and widens QRS duration.

Phase 1 is early repolarization, caused by transient outward potassium current, creating the action potential "notch."

Phase 2 is the plateau phase, where calcium influx through L-type calcium channels is balanced by potassium efflux, maintaining the membrane potential near 0 mV. This phase corresponds to the ST segment on ECG. Calcium influx during this phase triggers cardiac muscle contraction through excitation-contraction coupling. Calcium channel blockade reduces contractility and slows conduction in SA and AV nodes.

Phase 3 is repolarization, where potassium efflux through delayed rectifier potassium channels returns the membrane potential to -90 mV. This phase corresponds to the T wave on ECG. Potassium channel blockade prolongs repolarization and extends the QT interval, which can increase risk of Torsades de Pointes.

### Ion Channels and ECG Correlation

Sodium channels determine depolarization speed and correlate with QRS width. Sodium channel blockers widen the QRS complex, and severe hyperkalemia also affects sodium channel function.

L-type calcium channels affect plateau duration and SA/AV node conduction, correlating with heart rate and PR interval. Calcium channel blockers slow heart rate and prolong PR interval.

Potassium channels (IKr, IKs) determine repolarization time and correlate with QT interval. Potassium channel blockers prolong QT, and hypokalemia also prolongs QT by reducing IKr current.

---

## 3. Vaughan-Williams Classification

The Vaughan-Williams classification categorizes antiarrhythmic drugs by their primary mechanism of action on cardiac ion channels. Understanding this classification helps predict ECG effects and clinical applications of each drug class.

### Class I: Sodium Channel Blockers

Class I drugs block voltage-gated sodium channels, reducing phase 0 depolarization velocity. The primary ECG effect is QRS widening.

Class Ia agents (quinidine, procainamide, disopyramide) provide moderate sodium blockade plus potassium channel blockade, prolonging action potential duration. They widen both QRS and QT intervals.

Class Ib agents (lidocaine, mexiletine) provide weak sodium blockade and shorten action potential duration in ischemic tissue. They have minimal effect on QRS width.

Class Ic agents (flecainide, propafenone) provide strong sodium blockade without affecting action potential duration. They cause marked QRS widening. These agents are contraindicated in structural heart disease due to proarrhythmic risk demonstrated in the CAST trial.

### Class II: Beta-Adrenergic Blockers

Class II drugs block beta-adrenergic receptors, reducing sympathetic stimulation and slowing SA and AV node conduction. The primary ECG effects are decreased heart rate and prolonged PR interval. Examples include metoprolol, atenolol, propranolol, esmolol, carvedilol, and bisoprolol. Clinical applications include rate control in atrial fibrillation, SVT prevention, post-MI mortality reduction, and heart failure with reduced ejection fraction.

### Class III: Potassium Channel Blockers

Class III drugs block potassium channels (primarily IKr), prolonging phase 3 repolarization and the refractory period. The primary ECG effect is QT interval prolongation. Examples include amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide, ibutilide, and dronedarone.

These agents carry risk of Torsades de Pointes with excessive QT prolongation. QTc and electrolytes (potassium, magnesium) should be monitored. Amiodarone has properties of all four classes and is associated with multiple organ toxicities affecting thyroid, pulmonary, hepatic, and corneal tissues.

### Class IV: Calcium Channel Blockers

Class IV drugs block L-type calcium channels, reducing phase 2 plateau and slowing SA and AV node conduction. The primary ECG effects are decreased heart rate and prolonged PR interval. The relevant agents are verapamil and diltiazem (non-dihydropyridines). Clinical applications include rate control in atrial fibrillation, SVT termination, and SVT prevention.

Contraindications include pre-excited atrial fibrillation (WPW), heart failure with reduced ejection fraction, hypotension, and concurrent IV beta-blocker use.

### Class V: Other Mechanisms

Adenosine activates A1 receptors, opening potassium channels and hyperpolarizing SA and AV nodes. It is first-line for acute SVT termination.

Digoxin inhibits sodium-potassium ATPase, increasing vagal tone and slowing AV nodal conduction. It is used for rate control in atrial fibrillation, especially with heart failure.

Magnesium sulfate stabilizes cell membranes and suppresses early afterdepolarizations. It is first-line treatment for Torsades de Pointes.

# Part 2: ECG Interpretation Fundamentals

## Overview

The electrocardiogram (ECG) is the cornerstone diagnostic tool for cardiac arrhythmias. Mastery requires understanding basic ECG components and intervals, followed by a systematic approach to rhythm interpretation. This foundation enables accurate diagnosis and appropriate treatment selection.

---

## Basic ECG Components

### P Wave

The P wave represents atrial depolarization. It should be upright in leads I, II, and aVF, with duration less than 120 ms and amplitude less than 2.5 mm. Absent P waves suggest atrial fibrillation, junctional rhythm, or ventricular rhythm. Abnormal P wave morphology indicates atrial enlargement. Inverted P waves suggest an ectopic atrial focus or retrograde conduction.

### PR Segment

The PR segment represents AV nodal conduction delay, appearing as an isoelectric period from the end of the P wave to the start of the QRS complex. This delay allows atrial contraction to complete before ventricular filling.

### QRS Complex

The QRS complex represents ventricular depolarization. Normal duration is less than 120 ms (less than 3 small boxes). Wide QRS complexes (≥120 ms) are seen in bundle branch block, hyperkalemia, sodium channel blocker toxicity, ventricular origin rhythms, and pre-excitation syndromes such as WPW.

### ST Segment

The ST segment represents early ventricular repolarization, appearing as an isoelectric period from the end of the QRS to the start of the T wave. ST elevation indicates acute MI (STEMI) or pericarditis. ST depression indicates ischemia or reciprocal changes.

### T Wave

The T wave represents ventricular repolarization. It is normally upright in most leads, inverted in aVR, and variable in leads III, aVL, and V1. Peaked T waves suggest hyperkalemia. Inverted T waves indicate ischemia. T wave changes are seen in various electrolyte abnormalities.

### U Wave

The U wave represents late repolarization, possibly from Purkinje fibers or M cells. It appears as a small positive deflection after the T wave, most prominent in leads V2-V4. Prominent U waves suggest hypokalemia and may also be seen with bradycardia.

---

## Key Intervals

### PR Interval

The PR interval measures conduction time from atria through AV node to ventricles, measured from P wave onset to QRS onset. Normal range is 120-200 ms (3-5 small boxes). Prolonged PR interval (>200 ms) indicates first-degree AV block. Shortened PR interval (<120 ms) with delta wave indicates pre-excitation (WPW). Common causes of PR prolongation include beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers (verapamil, diltiazem), digoxin, and amiodarone.

### QRS Duration

QRS duration measures ventricular depolarization speed. Normal duration is less than 120 ms (less than 3 small boxes). Widened QRS indicates conduction delay or ventricular origin rhythm. Common causes of QRS widening include Class IC drugs (flecainide, propafenone), tricyclic antidepressants, severe hyperkalemia, and bundle branch block.

### QT and QTc Interval

The QT interval measures total ventricular electrical activity (depolarization plus repolarization) from QRS onset to T wave end. Because QT varies with heart rate, the corrected QT (QTc) must be calculated using the Bazett formula: QTc equals QT divided by the square root of the RR interval in seconds.

Normal QTc is less than 450 ms in men and less than 460 ms in women (some guidelines use less than 470 ms for women). QTc greater than 500 ms indicates high risk for Torsades de Pointes.

Common causes of QT prolongation include antipsychotics, fluoroquinolones, macrolides, Class Ia and III antiarrhythmics, antiemetics, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, and hypocalcemia.

---

## Systematic ECG Interpretation: The R-R-P-Q-I Method

A systematic approach ensures no critical findings are missed. The R-R-P-Q-I method provides a structured framework: Rate, Rhythm, P waves, QRS width, and Intervals.

### Step 1: Calculate Rate

For regular rhythms, use the 300 rule: count the number of large boxes between two R waves and divide 300 by that number. Quick reference: 1 box equals 300 bpm, 2 boxes equals 150 bpm, 3 boxes equals 100 bpm, 4 boxes equals 75 bpm, and 5 boxes equals 60 bpm.

For irregular rhythms, use the 6-second method: count QRS complexes in 6 seconds (30 large boxes) and multiply by 10.

Interpretation: less than 60 bpm is bradycardia, 60-100 bpm is normal, and greater than 100 bpm is tachycardia.

### Step 2: Assess Rhythm

Examine R-R intervals for regularity. Regular rhythm shows consistent R-R intervals throughout, seen in sinus rhythm, SVT, atrial flutter with fixed block, and VT. Irregularly irregular rhythm shows R-R intervals that vary without any pattern, which strongly suggests atrial fibrillation. Regularly irregular rhythm shows a predictable pattern of irregularity, seen in second-degree AV block (Mobitz I) and bigeminal PACs or PVCs.

### Step 3: Examine P Waves

Ask three questions about P waves. First, are they present? Is there a P wave before every QRS? Absent P waves suggest AF, junctional rhythm, or ventricular rhythm. Second, are they upright? An upright P wave in lead II indicates normal sinus origin. Inverted P waves suggest ectopic atrial rhythm or retrograde conduction. Third, are they consistent? Variable P wave morphology suggests wandering atrial pacemaker or multifocal atrial tachycardia.

### Step 4: Measure QRS Width

Narrow QRS (less than 120 ms) indicates supraventricular origin, meaning the impulse travels normally through the His-Purkinje system. Wide QRS (≥120 ms) indicates ventricular origin OR supraventricular rhythm with aberrant conduction or pre-excitation. The clinical rule is to assume VT until proven otherwise for wide complex tachycardia, as this is the safer approach.

### Step 5: Measure Intervals

Assess PR interval (P start to QRS start) for AV conduction. Prolonged PR suggests AV block; shortened PR with delta wave suggests pre-excitation. Assess QT/QTc interval (QRS start to T wave end) for repolarization. Prolonged QTc increases Torsades risk and is important for drug safety monitoring.

---

## Tachyarrhythmia Recognition (HR > 100 bpm)

### Narrow QRS Tachycardias

Narrow QRS, regular, no visible P waves suggests AVNRT. P waves are buried in the QRS complex, rate is 150-250 bpm, and pseudo r' may be seen in V1 or pseudo S in inferior leads.

Narrow QRS, regular, retrograde P waves suggests AVRT. P waves appear after the QRS, there may be a delta wave on baseline ECG (WPW), and RP interval is greater than 70 ms.

Narrow QRS, irregularly irregular, no P waves indicates atrial fibrillation. There is a chaotic fibrillatory baseline, no identifiable P waves, and completely variable R-R intervals.

Narrow QRS with sawtooth pattern indicates atrial flutter. Flutter waves occur at 250-350 per minute, typically with 2:1 block resulting in ventricular rate around 150 bpm.

### Wide QRS Tachycardias

Wide QRS, regular suggests VT or SVT with aberrancy. Assume VT as the safer approach. AV dissociation, fusion beats, and capture beats favor VT. Brugada criteria help differentiate.

Wide QRS, irregular, with varying morphology indicates pre-excited AF (WPW). This is a dangerous condition that may degenerate to VF. Avoid all AV nodal blockers including adenosine, verapamil, diltiazem, digoxin, and beta-blockers.

---

## Bradyarrhythmia Recognition (HR < 60 bpm)

Normal P-QRS-T with slow rate indicates sinus bradycardia. There is a P wave before every QRS with normal PR interval (120-200 ms). This may be physiological in athletes or during sleep.

Prolonged PR (>200 ms) with all P waves conducted indicates first-degree AV block. This is benign but should prompt monitoring for drug causes. There are no dropped beats.

Progressive PR lengthening followed by dropped QRS indicates second-degree Mobitz I (Wenckebach). This is often benign, commonly caused by vagal tone or drugs. There is grouped beating with R-R intervals progressively shortening before the pause.

Constant PR with intermittent dropped QRS indicates second-degree Mobitz II. This is more serious as it represents infranodal block with risk of progression to complete block. Pacemaker is often required.

P waves and QRS complexes dissociated with slow ventricular rate indicates third-degree (complete) AV block. Atria and ventricles beat independently. Escape rhythm determines rate. Permanent pacemaker is usually required.

No P waves with slow irregularly irregular rhythm indicates slow atrial fibrillation. Assess for drug toxicity (digoxin, beta-blockers, CCBs). This may indicate AV node disease or excessive rate control.

No P waves with slow regular rhythm indicates junctional escape rhythm. Rate is 40-60 bpm. Assess for drug toxicity. This may occur with SA node dysfunction.

# Part 3: Cardiac Arrhythmia Overview

## Definition

Cardiac arrhythmia refers to any irregular rate or rhythm in the electrical activity of the heart. Arrhythmias result from abnormalities in impulse generation (automaticity), impulse conduction, or both. They range from benign (such as sinus arrhythmia) to life-threatening (such as ventricular fibrillation).

---

## Normal Heart vs. Arrhythmia

In the normal heart, the sinus node generates regular impulses at 60-100 bpm. Signals propagate steadily to the AV node, ensuring coordinated atrial and ventricular contraction. Regular R-R intervals appear on ECG.

In arrhythmia, there is chaotic or abnormal signal generation. Taking atrial fibrillation as an example, multiple abnormal impulses arise in the atria. The atria cannot contract effectively. The ventricular response becomes irregular, appearing as irregularly irregular R-R intervals with no identifiable P waves on ECG.

---

## Classification of Arrhythmias

### By Heart Rate

Bradyarrhythmias (HR < 60 bpm) include sinus bradycardia, sinus node dysfunction (sick sinus syndrome), AV blocks (first, second, and third degree), and junctional or ventricular escape rhythms.

Tachyarrhythmias (HR > 100 bpm) include sinus tachycardia, supraventricular tachycardia (AVNRT, AVRT, atrial tachycardia), atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, ventricular tachycardia, and ventricular fibrillation.

### By Origin

Supraventricular arrhythmias originate above the ventricles and usually produce narrow QRS complexes (less than 120 ms). These include sinus arrhythmias, atrial arrhythmias (AF, AFL, AT, MAT), junctional arrhythmias (AVNRT), and accessory pathway-mediated arrhythmias (AVRT).

Ventricular arrhythmias originate from the ventricles and produce wide QRS complexes (≥120 ms). These include premature ventricular contractions (PVCs), ventricular tachycardia, ventricular fibrillation, and idioventricular rhythm.

### By Mechanism

Abnormal automaticity involves enhanced or abnormal spontaneous depolarization of pacemaker or non-pacemaker cells. Examples include sinus tachycardia, ectopic atrial tachycardia, and accelerated idioventricular rhythm.

Reentry occurs when electrical impulses circulate in a closed loop due to dual pathways with different conduction velocities and refractory periods. Requirements include two distinct pathways, unidirectional block in one pathway, and slow conduction in the alternate pathway. Examples include AVNRT, AVRT, atrial flutter, scar-related VT, and most AF.

Triggered activity involves afterdepolarizations that trigger abnormal impulses. Early afterdepolarizations (EADs) occur during phase 2 or 3, are caused by prolonged action potential (long QT), and can trigger Torsades de Pointes. Delayed afterdepolarizations (DADs) occur during phase 4, are caused by intracellular calcium overload, and are seen in digitalis toxicity and CPVT.

Conduction abnormalities involve block or delay in normal conduction pathways. Examples include SA exit block, AV blocks, and bundle branch blocks.

---

## Common Causes of Arrhythmias

Structural heart disease is the most common cause and includes coronary artery disease, myocardial infarction (causing scar-related VT), cardiomyopathies (dilated, hypertrophic, arrhythmogenic), valvular heart disease, congenital heart disease, and heart failure.

Electrolyte abnormalities include hypokalemia, hyperkalemia, hypomagnesemia, hypocalcemia, and hypercalcemia. These alter ion gradients and action potential characteristics.

Medications and toxins include QT-prolonging drugs (antipsychotics, antibiotics, antiarrhythmics), digitalis toxicity, sympathomimetics (cocaine, amphetamines), and alcohol (holiday heart syndrome).

Metabolic and endocrine causes include hyperthyroidism, hypothyroidism, hypoxia, acidosis, fever, and sepsis.

Autonomic causes include increased sympathetic tone (stress, pain, anxiety) and increased vagal tone (athletes, sleep).

Genetic and channelopathy causes include long QT syndrome, Brugada syndrome, catecholaminergic polymorphic VT (CPVT), and Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome. These represent inherited defects in cardiac ion channels.

Other causes include idiopathic fibrosis of the conduction system (aging), inflammatory conditions (myocarditis, pericarditis), infiltrative diseases (amyloidosis, sarcoidosis), and post-cardiac surgery.

---

## Clinical Presentation

### Common Symptoms

Palpitations are sensations of rapid, fluttering, or pounding heartbeat. Dizziness and lightheadedness result from reduced cardiac output. Syncope and near-syncope represent transient loss of consciousness from cerebral hypoperfusion. Dyspnea is shortness of breath, especially with exertion or rapid rates. Chest discomfort may mimic angina, especially with rapid ventricular rates. Fatigue results from reduced cardiac efficiency and is common in chronic AF.

### Serious Presentations

Hemodynamic instability presents with hypotension (SBP < 90 mmHg), altered mental status, and signs of shock. Acute heart failure presents with pulmonary edema, severe dyspnea, and tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy. Stroke and TIA result from thromboembolism from AF and may be the first presentation. Cardiac arrest includes pulseless VT, VF, asystole, and PEA, requiring immediate CPR and defibrillation.

### Asymptomatic Arrhythmias

Many arrhythmias are discovered incidentally during routine examination or monitoring. Silent AF affects up to 30% of patients and may only be diagnosed after stroke occurs.

---

## Management Principles

### Acute Management

Step 1 is to assess hemodynamic stability. Unstable patients require immediate intervention with cardioversion or defibrillation.

Step 2 is to identify and treat reversible causes including electrolytes, ischemia, drugs and toxins, hypoxia, and acid-base abnormalities.

Step 3 is the rate versus rhythm control decision, based on arrhythmia type, symptoms, hemodynamics, and patient factors.

Step 4 is specific pharmacological or electrical intervention, with drug selection based on arrhythmia mechanism and patient comorbidities.

### Long-term Management

Risk stratification involves assessing stroke risk (CHA₂DS₂-VASc for AF) and sudden cardiac death risk.

Anticoagulation for AF and AFL is based on stroke risk, with DOACs preferred over warfarin in most cases.

Antiarrhythmic drug therapy selection is based on Vaughan-Williams classification and patient factors.

Catheter ablation is curative for many SVTs and increasingly used for AF and VT.

Device therapy includes pacemakers for bradyarrhythmias and ICDs for VT/VF prevention.

Risk factor modification addresses hypertension, obesity, sleep apnea, alcohol, and diabetes.
